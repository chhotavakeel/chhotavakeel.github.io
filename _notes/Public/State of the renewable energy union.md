[[All electricity is accounting]], I'd said some time ago, and while I suppose that's still largely true, the process of actually getting to the [[electricity]] stage is looking a little more uncertain. "Electron sources tend to react badly to annoying real-world stimuli like geopolitics and the environment", I'd said at the time, and that definitely holds true for 2026. Currently, as we speak, there's some sort of conflict going on between the United States and Iran, which may or may not involve [[oil]], prediction markets, and the Armageddon.

And so, we live in interesting times, where the fate of the world's largest oil shipping lane[^1] hangs in the balance. Which puts [[renewable energy]] in an interesting place, if you think about it.

Ideally, the conflict ends this week, without any major repercussions. And then congratulations - the world gets a jump scare, but oil flows continue and it's business as usual. Hopefully, everyone sees the wisdom in continuing to increase renewables' share in their energy mix - this time, to reduce dependence on oil imports. This makes the energy transition more a matter of energy, and therefore, by extension, national security, rather than something stemming from environmental considerations. I see this as a good thing!

If not, then yes, oil is [voluminous and oozy and poisonous and flammable and smelly](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2020-04-20/money-stuff-there-s-nowhere-to-put-the-oil), but also used to power things that transport other things. And people. And so, because you need oil for transportation, and there isn't enough oil to go around, oil becomes more expensive. This makes transportation more expensive, which, in turn, makes everything much more expensive.

How does your central bank respond? By raising interest rates, hoping that an increase in the cost of borrowing will discourage spending, and therefore lower [[market]] demand. Unfortunately, this also has the unintended effect of discouraging investment in sunrise and/or capital-intensive sectors[^2] - driving up companies' costs of deploying capital makes cheaper or more lucrative investments much more attractive by comparison.

And so, even if you have the political will to add more renewable energy, you suddenly lack the financial capability to do so.

Which is unfortunate. And that, ladies and gentlemen, is the state of the renewable energy union in 2026.

[^1]: 20% of the world's global oil consumption flows through the Strait of Hormuz every year.

[^2]: Of which renewable energy is both.
